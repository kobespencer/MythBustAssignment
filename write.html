MythBusting: How does speaking 2 or more languages affect child development?

Language is the non-physical connection that humans interact with. Speaking is an integral part to what it means to be human and a part of society. In many ethnic households, children grow up learning two or more languages. Immigrant parents foster this kind of environment knowingly or unknowingly that it will help their child’s brain develop and prosper in the future. A Hungarian psychologist took this idea to the next level. Laszlo Polgar was able to raise three chess prodigies by following his method of raising geniuses. One requirement he mentioned was knowing two languages. The children all knew a widely spoken language in Hungary called Esperanto. These three children became chess record breakers. This experiment was definitely brought to the extreme but it suggested answer to the Nature vs. Nurture aspect of life. Polgar showed the world that healthy babies are able to become geniuses when given the right resources and parenting.

Karolina Lukasik et al set out to discover an answer to their hypothesis; Bilingual Executive Advantage (BEA). Their research method included testing individuals who were bilingual against those who were monolingual in working memory tests (Lukasik et al 2018). They had a sample size of 485 participants and were given tasks that tested their visuospatial working memory, verbal working memory, and n-back (n-back tests are tests that continuously test working memory). Some of the tests included their ability and frequency of switching between languages. The results were shocking and a lack of consistent support for the BEA hypothesis. The team ultimately found that the results were non-significant and any other tests were irrelevant to the BEA hypothesis.Their results were kind of inconclusive because they had small results showing that the bilinguals had a very small advantage for their working memory but unfortunately, not enough significance (Lukasik et al 2018). Their main defeat for the support of the BEA hypothesis was that “..our study failed to find consistent support for the BEA hypothesis in the domain of working memory. A major difficulty in this venue of research is that the original hypothesis does not delineate the exact conditions of bilingual experience that are required for BEA to emerge.” (Luksaik et al 2018).

While the team most likely did their best for their experiment; I find that there could be better ways of producing solid results for the BEA hypothesis. First, we are going to take a look at the fact that the BEA hypothesis could be expanded to not just cognitive function through tests, rather, the advantages overall. Advantages of being bilingual could come in many shapes and forms. Whether it involves traveling to other countries, interacting in the workplace, and or clearing up any language barriers, etc. Children and adults who know more than one language already seem two steps ahead of their peers who do not. For example, someone who is able to speak Chinese Mandarin or Spanish could potentially speak with the majority of the world. Secondly, Luksaik et al could have conducted their experiment in a different setting. Being bilingual in places such as the United States where culture and languages from all over the world meet, prove to be of an advantageous trait. To improve their experiment, I would add a survey research —this was mentioned briefly in their discussion portion of their paper— method to participants of different ethnic backgrounds. The survey would include how comfortable they are traveling to different countries, if the language they spoke has given them any advantage in the workplace, and if the language they speak has been a way of networking. This survey portion could potentially give insight to any day to day advantage one might overlook. Krista Byers-Heinlein and Casey Lew-Williams had similar things to say about this; “bilingual children will know multiple languages, which is important for travel employment, speaking with members of one’s extended family, maintaining a connection to a family culture and history..” (Heinlein 2018).

Finally, the BEA hypothesis is definitely something that will be studied and reviewed more often in the near future. Its “advantages” could extend and include moments in life where language is overlooked and not just some working memory test. Polgar raised geniuses through providing an environment that involved language learning and chess playing. Lukasik tried to study how knowing a second language might impact one’s working memory. Both of these people tried showing to the world what advantages being bilingual might have. While Lukasik et al was unable to fully prove their support for the BEA hypothesis, there could be more studies currently being done to find support and clearly show that bilingualism could prove to be an advantage growing up and later in life.
